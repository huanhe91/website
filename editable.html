The Racial Interface: Informatics and Asian/America

This project traces the co-evolution of race and informatics in the early digital era from 1942 to 1984. Shaped by midcentury sciences and design, informatics refers to the process of modeling, representing, and ultimately humanizing an enveloping world of data and digital infrastructure. I argue that the story of how information technology became integrated into the US mediascape parallels the myth of racial assimilation under US liberal capitalism. Analyzing visual culture from art, corporate, and government sources, The Racial Interface tracks what I call racial-technological schemas, arrangements of racial meaning and power that correspond with the humanization of the digital. Each chapter examines the interplay of racial and technological imaginaries in Asian/American and digital history, ranging from the cybernetic logics of Isamu Noguchis internment camp design to the computational origins of model minority discourse. Visual culture can bring to light the imperceptible operations of race and the digital, as their more liberal forms valorize these social phenomena on the representational level of skin and screen. Through Asian/American studies, I show how racial liberalisms experiments with agency, efficiency, and representation became bound to the rise of digital power in the 20th century.

I am a writer, educator, and poet based out of Los Angeles.

Currently, I am a Ph.D. candidate in American Studies & Ethnicity at the University of Southern California. I research at the intersection of race, technology, and visual culture.

My second project examines the Asiatic racialization of cheating in the rise of US liberal capitalism. Drawing from critical race studies, histories of labor and technology, and digital game studies, I locating the ludic across social and gaming phenomenon, from Chinese paper networks that hacked exclusionary immigration laws to Chinese virtual gold farmers in the multiplayer videogame World of Warcraft. Chinese/Cheaters demonstrate how the racial logic of cheating can both consolidate and challenge the liberal capitalist ethos of fair play.
